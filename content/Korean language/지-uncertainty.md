## Summary

**~지** is a [[dependent-noun]] that is placed at the end of a clause to indicate that the clause is contains an element of uncertainty, question, and/or guess. 

## Usage
**~지** is commonly used together with question words like 누구, 뭐, 언제, 어디, 왜, etc.:

- 엄마가 **누구**랑 먹는지 모르겠어요 
  I don’t know with **whom** mom is eating
- 엄마가 **뭐** 먹는지 모르겠어요
  I don’t know **what** mom is eating  
- 엄마가 **어디**서 먹는지 모르겠어요
  I don’t know **where** mom is eating  
- 엄마가 **왜** 먹는지 모르겠어요
  I don’t know **why** mom is eating

The final word of the sentence need not be `모드라`:
> 그 사람을 왜 데려오는지 물어봤어요
  I asked him why he is bringing that person

### Past tense
**~았/었** is added to the verb at the end of the uncertain clause, followed by **~는지**, to indicate uncertainty, a guess, or a question about something that has happened in the past:

- 저는 엄마가 왜 먹었는지 모르겠어요
  I don’t know why mom ate  
- 저는 엄마가 뭐 먹었는지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know what mom ate  
- 저는 엄마가 언제 먹었는지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know when mom ate  
- 저는 엄마가 어디서 먹었는지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know where mom ate  
- 저는 엄마가 밥을 먹었는지 모르겠어요
  I don’t know if mom ate

In speech, it is very common to hear **~ㄴ/은지** used instead:

- 저는 엄마가 왜 먹은지 모르겠어요
  I don’t know **why** mom ate  
- 저는 엄마가 뭐 먹은지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know **what** mom ate  
- 저는 엄마가 언제 먹은지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know **when** mom ate  
- 저는 엄마가 어디서 먹은지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know **where** mom ate  
- 저는 엄마가 밥을 먹은지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know **if** mom ate

**~았/었는지** is the correct grammatical form. **~ㄴ/은지** is more common in casual speech.

### Future tense
The same concept can be used to indicate a guess, question or uncertainty in the future tense by attaching **~ㄹ/을** to the verb at the end of the uncertain clause, followed by **~지**:

- 택배가 언제 올지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know when the delivery will come  
- 용돈을 얼마나 줄지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know how much allowance I should give  
- 오후에 비가 올지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know if it will rain in the afternoon  
- 수술을 받을지 확실하지 않아요요
  It is not certain if I will get surgery  
- 내일 공원에 갈지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know if I will go to the park tomorrow  
- 내일 영화를 볼지 모르겠어요
  I don’t know if I will see a movie tomorrow

When the uncertain clause contain a question word, it is common to use the word “might” in the English translation:

- 오후에 비가 올지 모르겠어요
  It might rain in the afternoon tomorrow  
- 수술을 받을지 모르겠어요요
  I might get surgery  
- 내일 공원에 갈지 모르겠어요요
  I might go to the park tomorrow  
- 내일 영화를 볼지 모르겠어요요
  I might see a movie tomorrow

### With adjectives
Using this grammar with adjectives is done by adding **~ㄴ/은지** to the adjective:

- 제가 준 것이 괜찮은지 모르겠어요]
  I don’t know if the thing that I gave is good  
- 이 빛이 충분히 밝은지 모르겠어요]
  I don’t know if this light is bright enough  
- 제가 구한 아르바이트가 좋은지 모르겠어요]
  I don’t know if the job I found is good  
- 제가 가져온 자료가 충분한지 모르겠어요]
  I don’t know if I brought enough materials  
- 제가 강아지를 기르고 싶은지 모르겠어요]
  I don’t know if I want to raise a puppy  
- 그 책이 얼마나 긴지 모르겠어요]
  I don’t know how long that book is

With past or future tense:

- 그 시대가 그렇게 길었는지 깨닫지 못했어요
  I didn’t realize that era was so long  
- 그 일이 힘들지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know if that work will be difficult  
- 내일 날씨가 쌀쌀할지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know if tomorrow’s weather will be chilly

### If… or not…
It is possible to indicate more than one situation by using more than one verb or adjective connected to ~는지 in the sentence:

- 내일 영화를 볼지 안 볼지 모르겠어요
  I don’t know if I will see a movie tomorrow or not  

When dealing with non-하다 verbs (like 먹다), you need to write out the verb again to indicate “I don’t know if mom ate **or not**.” However, when dealing with 하다 verbs, the sentence can usually be shortened by eliminating the word before ~하다 the second time. For example:

> 저는 엄마가 공부했는지 안 공부했는지 모르겠어요

You could just say:

- 저는 엄마가 공부했는지 안 했는지 모르겠어요요
  I don’t know if mom studied or not

Remember that Korean people love shortening their sentences, and taking out the redundant “공부” the second time around is more natural in Korean.

In all of the above examples, two possibilities are listed, and the speaker is indicating that he/she doesn’t know which one will happen amongst the two. The examples above simply use the positive and negative outcomes of the same situation. It is also possible to list two (or more) outcomes that are unrelated to each other. For example:

- 내일 공원에 갈지 영화를 볼지 모르겠어요]
  I don’t know if I will see a movie or go to the park tomorrow

Sometimes, **지** represents something certain:

- 서울에 어떻게 가는지 알아요
  I know how to get to Seoul  
  
In those examples, “지” technically doesn’t represent something uncertain…. so why do we use “지?” In these cases, the use of the question word in the sentence makes it more natural to use “지” as the noun instead of “것.”

Another way to express that one knows how do to something is [[~(ㄹ)을, (ㄴ)은 줄 알다, 모르다]]. This grammar is based more on ability than knowing something.

### ~지 with ~도
**~도** can be added to uncertain clauses conjugated in the future tense to express uncertainty as to whether something will happen in the future _or not_. When used with clauses conjugated in the past or present tenses, the meaning of **~도** will usually align with that discussed in [[~도]].

**~도** is _not_ added to uncertain clauses where there is a question word in the clause. The following sentences are awkward both in Korean and English:

> 비가 언제 올지도 모르겠다.
> I don't know when it will rain or not.

### Have been doing X for Y – 지
This usage represents the time period from when the action took place until the present.
While mostly straightforward, as is often the case with Korean grammar the exact meaning is dependent on context:

> 밥을 먹은 지 5분 됐다

All of the following are permissible translations:

> I have been eating for five minutes  
> It has been five minutes since I started/have been eating
> I haven’t eaten for five minutes  
> It has been five minutes since I last ate

If the action is still occurring, the first two translations would be more appropriate, and vice versa if the action has concluded.
